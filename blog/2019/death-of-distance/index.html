Has Globalisation Brought About the Death of Distance?

Created on January 02, 2019

2019   ·   globalisation   geography   inequality   trade   distance   ·   essays

Written as part of GY 409 – Globalisation and Regional Development, MSc Local Economic Development, London School of Economics and Political Science (2018–2019).

“The Death of Distance”, “The World is Flat” or “The Borderless World” - commentators in the financial and economic press have found metaphors aplenty to describe the levelling effects of globalisation. And who could deny that the advances in transport and communication technology have brought humanity closer together? Goods and services are traded across the globe, firms have internationalised, and the spread of the internet allows people to communicate in real time despite being thousands of miles away from each other. Beyond that, numerous trade agreements and supranational organisations have led to an unprecedented harmonisation of policies and regulations across countries. As a result, the sovereignty of the Westphalian nation-state has come under fire as the world ushered an era of “hyperglobalisation”. Hence, it seems straightforward to argue that globalisation has brought about the “death of distance”. But I will argue that this judgment was premature and lacks rigour. On closer inspection, distance still matters a great deal and globalisation has not brought about its death.

To begin with, let us look at the two most important ways in which globalisation might have reduced the effect of distance. First, the past 100 years have seen massive improvements in technology which have reduced costs for transport and communication substantially and integrated hitherto unconnected parts of the world into the global economy. The cost of shipping goods around the globe has reduced by more than 70% since 1920 (Combes et al., 2008). The number of airline passengers has increased from 310 million in 1970 to 4.4 billion in 2019. Finally, the global share of individuals using the internet has skyrocketed from 6% in 2000 to 49% in 2017. These trends are not exclusive to high-income countries. In fact, internet usage in Kenya is now 70 times higher than it was in the early 2000s (World Bank, 2021). Second, trade and financial liberalisation as well as the foundation of supranational organisations have pushed back nation-state sovereignty and brought about institutional levelling. The foundation of the WTO in 1995 extended trade liberalisation far beyond tariff reductions and led to a harmonisation of regulations in areas such as intellectual property rights or health and safety standards (Rodrik, 2018). Financial deregulation and the abolition of capital controls globalised financial markets and meant that investors and stockbrokers were now longer constrained to their domestic economies. These harmonisation efforts found their apex in the EU, where the completion of the “Single Market” project effectuated the “four freedoms of movement” – goods, services, people, and money (Chang, 2014). Indeed, it seems as if both forces worked their magic to reduce the effect of distance. There has been a substantial increase in trade volumes and FDI flows since the 1990s. Moreover, increased mobility and communication have contributed to the dispersion of ideas, knowledge, and technology. So, has globalisation really brought about the death of distance? Not so fast. If that was the case, why don’t we see rapid convergence in technological innovation and living standards across the world? Why do the chances of benefitting from globalisation still vary so widely for different economies and different individuals (Rodríguez-Pose, 2008)? Because distance still matters in the 21st century – let me explain why.

It is a well-established fact in the economics literature that trade, FDI and capital flows decrease with distance. It has been estimated that a 10 percent increase in distance reduces trade volumes by 10 percent. Perhaps surprisingly, a study by Disdier and Head finds that the negative effect of distance on trade has increased since the 1960s (Disdier, 2008). This finding demonstrates two things. First, despite the reduction of transport and communication costs and substantial efforts to harmonise regulations, transaction costs resulting from linguistic and cultural differences remain stubbornly high. Second, there has been a profound economic shift towards more innovative and creative industries in which business networks and relationships arising from proximity matter a great deal. Except for a few long-established manufacturing sectors such as textiles, furniture and simple consumer goods, industries are not nearly as “footloose” as has been suggested. As Ed Leamer puts it, distance still matters because it “creates cost-advantaged relationships between sellers and buyers located close to one another” (Leamer, 2007). The draught horses of the global economy cluster in a few regions with favourable conditions and draw on the benefits of agglomeration economies, thereby increasing the effects of distance. Innovation in the pharmaceutical sector and the software industry, two sectors that have benefitted massively from globalisation, requires not only highly skilled workers and good institutions but trust and cooperation - things that are still much harder to build across the globe than with co-workers next door. As a result, patenting is more concentrated than ever before. And while numerous Asian countries have experienced substantial increases in living standards, least developed countries, particularly in sub-Saharan Africa were virtually left out. At the same time, interpersonal and regional inequalities within countries have increased across the globe (Bourguignon, 2015). On the political front, the “death of distance” narrative often went hand in hand with a swansong of the nation-state. This too has been belied by the facts. According to the World Values Survey, people across the world still feel much more attached to their respective nations than to the global community. This holds true even for the EU, despite all the efforts put into forging a European identity. Finally, the place of birth remains the most important determinant for predicting a person’s income and explains 60% of the total variation in global income levels (Clemens, 2008).

In conclusion, I have demonstrated that globalisation has not brought about the proclaimed “death of distance”. Undoubtedly, globalisation has connected people across the globe. It also contributed to pull millions of people out of poverty. And yes, it has reduced the distance between a few select metropolitan areas across the globe - connecting Mumbai and Beijing with London and San Francisco. But for people in rural India, Western China and the American Midwest, these places seem more distant than ever before.
