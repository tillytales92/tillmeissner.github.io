Populism and Deindustrialisation: Trade Liberalisation or Technological Change?

Created on January 01, 2019

2019   ·   globalisation   populism   trade   technology   inequality   ·   essays

Written as part of GY 409 – Globalisation and Regional Development, MSc Local Economic Development, London School of Economics and Political Science (2018–2019).

The rise of right-wing populism in high-income countries is one of the most pressing issues of our time. Donald Trump, the Brexit advocates, and right-wing parties like the AfD in Germany have all build their campaigns on anti-immigration and anti-globalisation rhetoric. What explains their success? Empirical evidence suggests that support for populist parties is particularly strong in regions that have suffered from declines in manufacturing employment and falling wages – a phenomenon that has been described as the revenge of the places that don’t matter (Rodríguez-Pose, 2018). There is, for example, Saxony - a lagging region of eastern Germany where the AfD obtained 27% of the votes in 2017. There also is West Virginia, a former industrial state in decline, where 68% of people voted for Donald Trump in 2020. These findings raise important questions about the reasons for the decline in manufacturing wages and employment. Are the anti-globalisation campaigners right to blame trade liberalisation? Or is technological change the more important force to consider?

For most of the 20th century, a strong manufacturing sector represented the backbone of economic success in Western Europe and Northern America. Especially during the boom years after World War II, the manufacturing sector spurred growth and created equitable wealth by employing large numbers of low-skilled workers productively. At its peak, during the 1960s, manufacturing employed around 40% of the total workforce in high-income countries. But the heyday of western manufacturing ended sometime during the 1970s, as two stylised facts suggest:

  1. Employment levels in manufacturing fell substantially in all high-income economies. For example, in the UK, the share of manufacturing employment fell from 22% in 1981 to 8% in 2019.
  2. Real wages of low-skilled manufacturing workers in high-income economies have declined between 1980 and 2019.

What explains this development? Trade liberalisation has become a major culprit in the political debate. At first glance, the underlying reasoning is compelling. The 1980s and 1990s saw significant reductions of trade barriers following the foundation of the World Trade Organization and the entrance of China and India into the world economy. As a result, the global labour market grew by millions of low-skilled workers offering their labour at very low wages. According to the classical Hecksher-Ohlin trade model, downward pressure on low-skilled manufacturing wages in high income economies is precisely what we should expect following these events (Bourguignon, 2015). For high-income economies, the model predicts that trade with low-income economies increases the returns to capital and high-skilled labour while it reduces the returns to low-skilled labour (Harrison et al., 2010). And indeed, China’s emergence to becoming a manufacturing powerhouse was promoted by offshoring of light manufacturing industries requiring low skills such as electronics assembly, textiles, and other consumer goods. While this generated substantial aggregate efficiency gains and raised consumer welfare in high-income economies, manufacturing industries at the lower end of the skills spectrum and the regions in which they were located were hit hard by greater exposure to imports. The effects were aggravated by the fact that labour market adjustments in terms of geographical and sectoral mobility were much more sluggish than economists had anticipated (Autor et al., 2016).

Does that mean anti-globalisation campaigners are right in putting all the blame for the decline of manufacturing employment on trade liberalisation? The short answer is no. There are empirical and conceptual issues with this narrative and I will argue that technological change plays a more important role. To begin with, this view neglects the fact that some manufacturing industries have benefitted substantially from the possibility to source inputs at low-cost from abroad bringing about positive aggregate employment effects. Think, for example, of the automotive industry in Germany. Secondly, manufacturing employment peaked during the 1960s and was therefore falling already long before China’s accession to the WTO in 2001 impacted trade volumes substantially. Most importantly however and often overlooked, is the fact that total manufacturing output in high-income economies has constantly grown, even in the United Kingdom, a country notorious for its declining productive sector. This means that a large part of the reduction in manufacturing employment is explained by increasing productivity because of technological innovation rather than import competition. In fact, technological innovation has been a double whammy for low-skilled workers in the Saxonies’ and the West Virginia’s of this world. It did not only reduce the number of manufacturing jobs, but it did so especially at the expense of workers without college degrees. This phenomenon is called skill-biased technological change and has led to increasing wage returns to human capital and job polarisation as a result (Katz, 2008). For the manufacturing sector, this means increasing demand for engineers and reduced demand and falling wages for workers undertaking routine tasks on the assembly line. Importantly, this is not to say that trade liberalisation did not play a role in this process. More competition on international markets increased pressure among manufacturing firms to innovate and thereby accelerated technological change (Bourguignon, 2015). But the point I am making is that technological change is the most important reason behind declining manufacturing employment and falling wages for low-skilled workers.

In conclusion, import competition did indeed have localised effects on certain sectors and regions. The grievances are real, and the socioeconomic consequences were particularly severe in countries with poor social safety nets like the United States. This might explain why trade with China played such a prominent role in Donald Trump’s campaign while it was absent in that of the AfD in Germany. Nevertheless, I have shown that manufacturing employment was on the decline a long time before trade with low-income countries intensified. Most importantly, the fact that total manufacturing output has been increasing across all high-income economies strongly suggests that technological innovation is the main reason behind the reduction in manufacturing employment. Therefore, to really help the places that don’t matter, the policy focus should be on promoting more inclusive technological innovation, improving education systems and strengthening social safety nets. Trumpist’ protectionism, in contrast, was a knee-jerk reaction to a problem poorly understood.
