Hobsbawm's magisterial account of the "short twentieth century" — from the outbreak of the First World War to the collapse of the Soviet Union — is one of the great works of modern historiography. The book covers this period in three sweeping movements: the Age of Catastrophe, the Golden Age, and the Landslide, and in doing so provides both a coherent narrative and a penetrating structural analysis of how capitalism, socialism, and empire shaped the world we live in.

What distinguishes this book is Hobsbawm's ability to hold the global and the local in tension simultaneously. He moves effortlessly between the grand sweep of geopolitics and the lived experience of ordinary people, and his prose never loses its analytical clarity. Written from the perspective of someone who lived through much of the century he describes, there is also a personal urgency to the argument.

Essential reading for anyone trying to understand the political and economic forces that continue to shape the present.
