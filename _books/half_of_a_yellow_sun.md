Adichie's second novel is set against the Biafran War (1967–1970) and follows three characters whose lives become entangled as Nigeria fractures: Ugwu, a village boy who becomes a houseboy in Enugu; Olanna, a beautiful and idealistic woman from a wealthy Lagos family; and Richard, an English journalist drawn to Nigeria by love. As the war tightens around them, Adichie traces both the grand scale of political catastrophe and the intimate scale of how people survive, betray, and endure it.

What distinguishes this novel is Adichie's refusal to let politics dwarf her characters. The war is always present but the novel is never reducible to it — it is also a story about desire, loyalty, class, and what people carry with them when everything else is gone. The prose is confident and precise, and the structure — which jumps between the early and late 1960s — is handled with exceptional skill.

One of the finest African novels of the past few decades.
