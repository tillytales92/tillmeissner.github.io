Published in 1972 and still essential half a century later, Rodney's argument is as clear as it is radical: African underdevelopment was not a natural condition or the product of internal failings, but the direct consequence of European colonialism and the slave trade. Europe did not merely fail to develop Africa — it actively underdeveloped it, extracting surplus that financed European industrialisation while systematically dismantling the economic, political, and social structures that had enabled African societies to function and grow on their own terms.

What makes this book so compelling is that Rodney combines rigorous historical analysis with moral clarity. He traces the mechanics of extraction — in agriculture, mining, taxation, and labour — and shows how colonial institutions were designed not to build African capacity but to prevent it. His chapter on the human capital costs of colonialism is particularly devastating.

Required reading for anyone working on African development, and a necessary corrective to the ahistorical framing that still dominates much of mainstream development economics.
