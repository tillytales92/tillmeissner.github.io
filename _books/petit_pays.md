A luminous and devastating novel told through the eyes of Gabriel, a Franco-Rwandan boy growing up in Burundi in the early 1990s. Faye captures childhood with extraordinary tenderness — the friendships, the mango trees, the small freedoms of a quiet neighbourhood — before the world fractures around him as civil war and the Rwandan genocide tear his family and country apart.

What makes this book exceptional is the precision of its emotional register. Faye never overdramatises; the horror arrives gradually, almost quietly, which makes it all the more devastating. The prose (originally in French) is spare and beautiful, and the child's perspective gives the political tragedy an intimacy that a more conventional historical novel could never achieve.

One of those rare books that stays with you long after the last page.
