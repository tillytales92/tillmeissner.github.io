Written before Obama entered politics and long before anyone imagined he would become president, this is a genuinely literary memoir — far more honest, searching, and formally ambitious than the genre usually allows. Obama writes about growing up between worlds: Hawaii and Indonesia, Black and white, American and Kenyan. The book is a slow, patient attempt to understand what it means to inherit a father you barely knew and an identity that others have already decided for you.

The three sections — childhood in Hawaii, community organising in Chicago, and a journey to Kenya to meet his father's family — are uneven in pace but remarkable throughout. The Chicago section is extraordinary: a close-grained portrait of a neighbourhood in decline and the limits of what idealism can achieve within institutions designed to contain it. The Kenya section is deeply moving, particularly the scenes at his father's grave.

Obama is a genuinely good writer — not merely "good for a politician" but good by any standard. A book that would be worth reading whoever had written it.
