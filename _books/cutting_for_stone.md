An enormous, generous novel that spans decades, continents, and generations — beginning in an Addis Ababa mission hospital in the 1950s and ending in New York. At its centre are conjoined twins Marion and Shiva, born in tragedy to a nun and a brilliant surgeon, and raised among the doctors, nurses, and patients of Missing Hospital. Verghese, himself a physician, writes about medicine with an intimacy and wonder that is genuinely rare.

The novel is unabashedly old-fashioned in the best possible sense: it believes in plot, in character, in the moral weight of a story told all the way through. Its portrait of Ethiopia — its landscape, its politics, the beauty and brutality of the Derg years — is vivid without being exploitative. And at its heart is a meditation on fathers and sons, on inheritance and betrayal, on what it means to be called to heal.

Long, ambitious, and deeply humane. A book that reminds you what novels are for.
