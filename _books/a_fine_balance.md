Set in India in the mid-1970s during Indira Gandhi's Emergency, this is one of the most devastating novels I have ever read — and one of the most extraordinary. Four strangers come to share a Mumbai apartment: a widow letting rooms, a student from a hill town, and two tailors from the untouchable caste who have fled caste violence in their village. Mistry traces their lives with a compassion and precision that makes each of them utterly irreducible.

The novel is unflinching about suffering — poverty, caste oppression, forced sterilisation, political violence — but it is never bleak for its own sake. Mistry insists on the dignity and resilience of his characters even as circumstances conspire against them, and the tenderness with which he treats them makes the tragedy, when it comes, all the more unbearable.

A long book that demands patience and gives everything back. Easily among the greatest novels written in English in the last half century.
